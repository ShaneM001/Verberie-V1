Executive Summary

Grimbergen — Strategic Overview

Grimbergen sits in the Halle-Vilvoorde arrondissement of Flemish Brabant, immediately north of Brussels and approximately 35 km south of Antwerp along the A12 motorway — part of the principal Brussels–Antwerp–Rotterdam freight corridor. Though the municipality itself has approximately 40,000 inhabitants, it anchors the northern industrial belt of the Brussels metropolitan area and sits at the convergence of road, rail and inland-waterway corridors serving a 120-minute catchment of over 22 million consumers spanning Belgium, the southern Netherlands (Rotterdam, Eindhoven, the Randstad), the Lille metropolitan area in northern France and the western Ruhr. The development site — Montea Grimbergen, the former Cokeworks business park — comprises approximately 100,000 m² of brownfield land sitting directly alongside the Brussels–Scheldt Sea Canal, which serves 11.8 million tonnes of goods annually and supports seagoing vessels of up to 9,000 tonnes; the site is 2-minute trucking time from a Van Moer Logistics barge container terminal, 6 minutes from a major Aldi distribution centre, and sits immediately alongside a Caterpillar facility. Multimodal connectivity is exceptional: the A12 (Brussels–Antwerp) runs immediately adjacent to the site, the R0 Brussels Ring is 5 minutes south, the E19/A1 Brussels–Antwerp main motorway is 7 minutes east via Vilvoorde, Brussels Airport 10 minutes east, and the Port of Antwerp-Bruges (Europe's second-largest port) approximately 35 minutes north — placing the ports of Antwerp, Zeebrugge and Rotterdam all within commercial reach. The proposed development comprises two warehouse structures, the larger configured as two separate rental units, both with a clear height of 12 metres and combined warehouse floorspace of approximately 51,000 m² (30,000 m² + 21,000 m²).

The cold-storage landscape around Grimbergen is markedly thin in the inner ring and increasingly dense — but ageing — at the catchment edge. Within a 30-minute drive there are just 2 sites totalling around 33,247 pallet positions across ~49,000 m², of which approximately 32,800 are dedicated frozen, with a weighted average age of 35 years — pointing to extremely limited modern in-catchment supply immediately adjacent to the Brussels food market. The 30–60 minute ring adds 17 further sites and 138,250 frozen pallets across ~265,000 m² at an average age of 19 years; the 60–90 minute band layers on the largest single tranche of capacity — 46 sites, 473,550 frozen pallets and ~661,000 m² of floorspace at an average age of 25; while the outer 90–120 minute ring is materially older, averaging 31 years across 63 sites and 237,200 frozen pallets, signalling a clear replacement and modernisation opportunity at the catchment edge. Benchmarked against the UK reference site at Magnavale Easton, the Grimbergen 120-minute catchment shows a strongly differentiated competitive profile: food-processor density is overwhelmingly stronger in several high-value cold-chain segments — dairy (616 vs 43, ~14×), eggs (500 vs 42, ~12×), fish & seafood (374 vs 99, ~4×), frozen products (611 vs 242, 2.5×) and meat (840 vs 547, 154%) — while it is broadly comparable in poultry (175 vs 164, 107%) and somewhat under-represented in ready meals (169 vs 200, 85%), fruit & vegetables (175 vs 189, 93%) and bakery (86 vs 108, 80%). Strategically, this is therefore a substantially under-supplied inner-ring market with overwhelming food-processor demand in dairy, egg, fish & seafood and frozen — segments where Belgium's structural position as a leading European dairy, protein and frozen-food producer drives consistent year-round throughput. The realistic opportunity sits in (i) capturing the chronic shortage of modern chilled and frozen capacity within the immediate 30-minute Brussels–Flemish Brabant ring where only two ageing sites currently operate, (ii) replacement demand from the materially older outer-ring stock, and (iii) anchoring the dairy, egg, fish & seafood and frozen processor density that meaningfully exceeds any UK benchmark.

Drivetime Analysis Summary

Grimbergen’s position immediately north of Brussels places it at the centre of one of Europe’s densest food-industry catchments. Within a 60-minute trucking-time radius the site reaches a population of 4–5 million and 582 food production and processing businesses; the 120-minute drive-time catchment extends to over 22 million people and 2,514 food businesses, spanning Belgium, the southern Netherlands, the Lille metropolitan area and the western Ruhr. The catchment density compares overwhelmingly favourably to UK reference points: against Magnavale Easton the Grimbergen 120-minute catchment captures 154% of meat businesses, 252% of frozen-food businesses, 378% of fish & seafood businesses, and over 12x the dairy and egg businesses, reflecting Belgium and the Netherlands’ dominance in continental European dairy and protein production. Existing cold storage capacity within 60 minutes is limited to just 19 sites with a weighted average construction date of ~2005 — pointing to a clear modernisation gap inside an otherwise exceptionally concentrated food-production geography.

Population Summary

Grimbergen is a municipality of approximately 40,000 inhabitants in the Halle-Vilvoorde arrondissement of Flemish Brabant, 10km north of Brussels — but its strategic geography places it at the centre of a 120-minute drive-time catchment of over 22 million people, extending across Belgium’s three regions, the southern Netherlands (Rotterdam, Eindhoven, the Randstad), the Lille metropolitan area in northern France and the western Ruhr. Within 60 minutes the catchment reaches 4–5 million people, encompassing the Brussels-Capital Region (~1.25M), the immediate Halle-Vilvoorde arrondissement (~673k) and the cities of Antwerp, Ghent, Leuven, Mechelen and Aalst. Flemish Brabant is Belgium’s wealthiest province by per-capita income (~€23,527, the highest of any Belgian province), with an unemployment rate of 3.5–4.1% (the lowest of any Belgian region), a Flemish employment rate of 76.9% (above the EU27 average) and a household budget allocation of approximately 16% to food and non-alcoholic beverages. This combination of a deep, affluent and economically active labour pool, the directly adjacent Brussels metropolitan food market, and exceptional motorway access via the immediately adjacent A12 and Brussels Ring R0 makes Grimbergen one of the most operationally favourable food-logistics locations in continental Europe.

Regional Agricultural Data

Agricultural Context & Supply Chain Overview

Belgium’s food industry is the country’s largest industrial sector, generating €85.1 billion in turnover, €42.4 billion in exports and 102,779 direct jobs in 2025 (Fevia) — making Belgium the 4th-largest food & drink exporter in the European Union despite its modest land area. Flanders dominates national output, accounting for 82% of food-industry turnover and approximately 68,400 direct food-industry jobs. The Flemish agricultural sector is intensively productive: 23,000 farms across an average of 27 hectares produce 46.8 million head of poultry, 5.7 million pigs and 1.3 million cattle, with livestock densities of 3.7 pigs and 0.87 cattle per hectare — among the highest in the European Union. Specialisation is high: 54% of Flemish farms focus on cattle and dairy, 21% on arable crops and 13% on horticulture, with the Pajottenland district of Flemish Brabant a recognised national dairy heartland generating year-round chilled-milk volumes within the immediate Grimbergen catchment.

For reference — Belgium ranks 4th in the European Union for food and drink exports, behind only the Netherlands, Germany and France, with €42.4 billion in food exports in 2025 representing approximately half of all sector turnover. Brussels sits at the geographic crossroads of the EU’s three largest food-producing nations and the Port of Antwerp-Bruges (the EU’s second-busiest port and the world’s second-largest port for fruit handling), positioning the Grimbergen catchment for both intra-EU food distribution and re-export. Belgium is internationally recognised for chocolate, beer, frozen French fries and processed dairy: the country hosts Callebaut (the world’s largest chocolate factory and warehouse), Greenyard (€4.2bn fruit & vegetable processor headquartered in Sint-Katelijne-Waver within the 30-minute catchment) and Belgium’s leading frozen-food and ice-cream operators. Manufacturing and logistics together represent the two largest FDI categories in Belgium (55 and 53 projects respectively in 2024), reflecting Belgium’s structural role as continental Europe’s distribution backbone.

The immediate Brussels – Flemish Brabant catchment hosts the densest concentration of food-industry assets in the Grimbergen drive-time area. Sint-Katelijne-Waver (within the 30-minute catchment) is home to Greenyard’s global headquarters and to BelOrta, Europe’s largest fruit and vegetable cooperative auction by traded volume. The Pajottenland district immediately west of Brussels remains the historic dairy heartland of Flemish Brabant, while the Antwerp province (north of the site) accounts for the highest concentration of specialised dairy farms in Flanders. Within a 120-minute trucking radius of Grimbergen the catchment hosts 840 meat businesses, 616 dairy businesses, 611 frozen-food businesses, 500 egg businesses and 374 fish & seafood businesses — each representing direct cold-chain customer prospecting opportunity for a modern facility on the site.

Investment Support Overview

Belgium’s inward investment offer is well-positioned for international food and logistics investors. In 2024 Belgium attracted 210 foreign direct investment projects (8th in the European rankings, ahead of Italy and the Nordic markets), creating 5,392 jobs, with manufacturing and logistics together representing 55 and 53 projects respectively — the two largest FDI categories. Within Belgium, Flanders consistently captures the majority share of foreign investment and food-sector projects, with Flanders Investment & Trade (FIT) providing a free, confidential one-stop-shop service covering site selection, financial incentive guidance, regulatory navigation and partner introductions. For the immediately adjacent Brussels-Capital Region, hub.brussels offers equivalent support, while VLAIO (the Flemish Agency for Innovation & Entrepreneurship) administers regional subsidies and innovation grants. Belgium’s headline corporate income tax rate of 25% is supplemented by the Innovation Income Deduction (reducing the effective tax rate on qualifying intellectual property income to 3.75%), the R&D tax credit, modernised investment deductions for environmentally friendly assets (14.5% one-off or 20.5% spread) and an enhanced 30.5% deduction for zero-carbon trucks and charging infrastructure — directly relevant to cold-chain logistics investment.

The Belgian food sector is exceptionally well represented at the federation level: Fevia (the Federation of the Belgian Food Industry) represents 27 sub-sector federations and over 700 companies with combined turnover of €85.1 billion and 102,779 direct employees — making Belgian food and drink the country’s largest industrial sector and largest industrial employer. The Brussels Wholesale Market (Mabru), located at Quai des Usines approximately 10–15 minutes from Grimbergen, is the Brussels-Capital Region’s primary fresh-produce wholesale hub — operating across 15 hectares, hosting 100+ wholesale and producer businesses, processing 10,000–15,000 tonnes of fresh produce per week, generating €600–800 million in annual on-site turnover and welcoming 22,000+ professional visitors per month. Combined with the Port of Antwerp-Bruges (Europe’s second-largest port, ~35 minutes north) and Brussels Airport (10 minutes east), Mabru represents an immediate, ready-made customer prospecting base spanning meat, fish, fruit & vegetable wholesalers and 3PL operators serving the Brussels – Flemish Brabant – southern Netherlands corridor.
