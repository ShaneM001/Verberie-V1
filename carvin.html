Executive Summary

Carvin — Strategic Overview

Carvin sits in the Pas-de-Calais department of Hauts-de-France, around 200 km north of Paris and just 25 km south of Lille along the A1 motorway — the principal Paris–Brussels–Amsterdam freight corridor. Though the commune itself has under 18,000 inhabitants, it anchors the Hénin-Carvin agglomeration and sits immediately adjacent to the Delta 3 multimodal platform at Dourges — northern France's largest combined rail–road–waterway logistics hub, spanning around 300 hectares and over 330,000 m² of Grade A warehousing already operated by tenants such as Décathlon, Kiabi, Dachser, Yusen Logistics (for Yamaha), Rexel, Leroy Merlin and Oxybul Éveil & Jeux. The site benefits from exceptional multimodal connectivity: direct A1 motorway access, an on-site combined transport terminal connecting to the Canal de la Deûle inland waterway and to the European rail network, 15 minutes from Lille-Lesquin airport, four TGV stations within 20 minutes (Lille, Lens, Douai, Arras), approximately 1 hour and 20 minutes from the Channel Tunnel, 2 hours from Paris-Charles de Gaulle, and 1h30 from Brussels-Zaventem. With the Seine-Nord Europe Canal scheduled to open in 2032 — linking the Seine basin to Belgium and the Netherlands via a 20,000-km wide-gauge waterway network — Carvin will sit at the convergence of road, rail, river and air corridors serving a 120-minute catchment of roughly 13–16 million consumers spanning Northern France, Belgium and the southern Netherlands, with the ports of Calais, Dunkirk, Antwerp and Rotterdam all within commercial reach.

The cold-storage landscape around Carvin is markedly deep, modern, and heavily concentrated in a small number of large-scale automated operators. Within a 30-minute drive there are 17 sites totalling around 311,800 pallet positions across ~153,000 m², of which 193,100 are dedicated frozen — and the bulk of that frozen capacity is concentrated in just two automated high-bay sites operated by Lineage Logistics at Harnes (around 10 km north-east of Carvin), each exceeding 70,000 frozen pallet positions and both delivered within the last 20 years. The inner ring's average age of just 8 years reflects this concentration of recent automated investment. The 30–60 minute ring adds 13 further sites and another 130,500 frozen pallets across ~188,000 m², now reaching into Belgian Flanders and Hainaut where the key automated players sit: Constellation Cold Logistics' Stockhabo "ICE" platform at Mouscron (the 2016 ICE facility plus two fully-automated high-bay extensions — HB1 at 60,000 and HB2 at 28,500 pallet positions — taking the site over 185,000 pallet positions in total just north of the French border), and Larfrost at the Lar transport hub in Rekkem (around 65,000 frozen europallet positions plus refrigerated and ambient overflow, directly off the E17/E403). The 60–90 minute band layers on the largest single tranche of capacity — 30 sites, 211,550 frozen pallets and ~324,000 m² of floorspace — while the outer 90–120 minute ring is materially older, averaging 24 years across 20 sites and 151,397 frozen pallets, pointing to a clear replacement and modernization opportunity at the catchment edge. Benchmarked against the UK reference site at Magnavale Easton, the Carvin 120-minute catchment shows a distinctive competitive profile: food-processor density is materially stronger in several high-value cold-chain segments — eggs (292 vs 42, ~7×), dairy (213 vs 43, ~5×), fish & seafood (196 vs 99, ~2×) and frozen products (308 vs 242, 127%) — while it is under-represented in meat (459 vs 547, 84%), poultry (131 vs 164, 80%), ready meals (85 vs 200, 43%), bakery (51 vs 108, 47%) and fruit & vegetables (53 vs 189, 28%). Strategically, this is therefore a capital-intensive, automation-led market: any new entrant will need to position against the Lineage Harnes / Stockhabo Mouscron / Larfrost Rekkem axis rather than enter an under-served catchment, with the realistic opportunity sitting in (i) replacement demand from the ageing 90–120 minute outer-ring stock, (ii) differentiated multi-temperature service propositions where the existing automated mega-sites are constrained by their single-temperature high-bay format, and (iii) capture of the dairy, egg, fish and frozen processor density that meaningfully exceeds the UK benchmark.

Drivetime Analysis Summary

Carvin's drivetime profile is exceptionally strong for a multi-temperature logistics site, reflecting its location at the convergence of three of Europe's busiest freight corridors. The site sits 5 minutes from the A1 (Paris–Lille), 18 minutes from the A27 (Lille–Brussels) and within easy reach of the A21, A26 and the cross-Channel network at Calais. A 60-minute trucking radius reaches 4–5 million consumers across the Lille–Lens–Douai–Valenciennes conurbation and into Belgian Flanders and Hainaut (Kortrijk, Tournai, Mouscron), while the 120-minute catchment extends to approximately 16 million consumers, encompassing virtually all of Hauts-de-France, the majority of Belgium (including Brussels and Antwerp), the northern fringes of Normandy and the southern Netherlands. Within that same two-hour radius the site reaches the deep-sea ports of Antwerp, Zeebrugge, Dunkirk and Calais, the Channel Tunnel at Coquelles, Lille-Lesquin airport (15 min) and four TGV high-speed rail stations (Lille, Lens, Douai, Arras) all within 20 minutes. Inland-waterway access via the Canal de la Deûle is on the doorstep and, with the Seine-Nord Europe Canal scheduled to open in 2032, Carvin will sit on a direct wide-gauge waterway connecting the Seine basin to Rotterdam and Antwerp — uniquely positioning the site for road, rail, river, sea and air freight within a single 30-minute service radius.

Population Summary

Carvin is a commune of approximately 17,900 inhabitants in the Pas-de-Calais department of Hauts-de-France, the 3rd most populous and 2nd most densely populated region in metropolitan France with around 6 million residents — but its strategic geography places it at the centre of a 120-minute drive-time catchment of roughly 16 million people spanning Hauts-de-France, the majority of Belgium and parts of the southern Netherlands, with 4–5 million reachable within 60 minutes. The commune sits within the Communauté d'agglomération d'Hénin-Carvin (14 communes, ~126,000 inhabitants) and the broader Arrondissement de Lens (~370,000), part of the dense post-mining urban basin stretching from Béthune through Lens, Douai and Valenciennes. The local economic profile reflects this industrial heritage rather than the affluent Paris-basin profile of comparable French logistics sites: average per-capita income at Carvin is €20,130 (just below the French national average of €20,590), unemployment is 10.4% (versus a national rate of 7.9% and a Hauts-de-France regional rate of ~9%), and the commune population is declining at approximately 0.3% per year. For a multi-temperature logistics operation, however, this profile is materially favourable: a deep, available labour pool, lower-than-national wage costs, and explicit regional and agglomeration-level public-sector positioning of logistics employment as the core economic-redevelopment lever following the closure of the coal mines — visible already in the Delta 3 multimodal platform at adjacent Dourges, which has anchored more than 330,000 m² of Grade A warehousing employing some of the region's largest blue-chip logistics tenants.

Regional Agricultural Data

Agricultural Context & Supply Chain Overview

Hauts-de-France is the agricultural anchor of northern France and one of the most intensively cultivated regions in Europe, with around 65% of its 31,813 km² territory in agricultural use. The region is the undisputed national leader in two of France's largest field-crop categories: it produces approximately 61% of French potatoes (Nord-Pas-de-Calais alone accounts for 38% and the former Picardie a further 23%), and it grows the largest single share of France's sugar beet, a crop on which France ranks 2nd globally with production of around 31 million tonnes annually. The Paris Basin's fertile limon soils extend across the southern half of the region, making Hauts-de-France a major contributor to French soft wheat, barley and rapeseed output, while the western and northern arcs support specialised vegetable production (endive, peas, beans, carrots), dairy cattle and pig farming. The result is the densest concentration of field-crop and livestock processing capacity in France, with downstream demand running through grain silos, sugar refineries, starch plants, frozen-vegetable lines, potato processors (McCain operates a major frozen-fries plant at adjacent Harnes, 3 km from Carvin) and dairy processors that together generate exceptional and persistent cold-chain throughput across the Carvin catchment.

For reference — France is the European Union's largest agricultural producer with annual output value of approximately €95 billion, and the country ranks among the global top 5 producers of wheat, barley, sugar beet and milk, and among the top 10 producers of potatoes. Nationally, agroalimentaire is the country's largest industrial sector by both turnover (€197 billion across 17,000+ companies represented by ANIA) and employment (450,000+ direct jobs). Within Carvin's 120-minute trucking radius food-processor density is materially stronger than at the UK reference site of Magnavale Easton across four high-value cold-chain segments — eggs (292 vs 42 processors, ~7×), dairy (213 vs 43, ~5×), fish & seafood (196 vs 99, ~2×) and frozen products (308 vs 242, 127%) — reflecting Boulogne-sur-Mer's role as France's largest fishing port and the principal European seafood-processing hub at Capécure, the dairy belt running from Flanders through Picardy, and the regional egg-products cluster anchored locally by PEP Carvin within the on-site catchment.

Belgium, the natural northern extension of the Carvin catchment, materially deepens this supply base. The country is a major European food exporter — particularly strong in frozen vegetables, where Belgian Flanders alone is estimated to account for roughly a quarter of global frozen-vegetable trade — alongside chocolate and confectionery, dairy, prepared meats and beer. Cross-border food flows between Hauts-de-France and Belgian Flanders/Hainaut are bidirectional and intensive: French producers ship through Antwerp and Zeebrugge for export, while Belgian processors (Ardo, Crop's, Greenyard, Lutosa, Mydibel, Clarebout) supply French retailers and foodservice operators through cross-docking and bonded cold-storage operations along the border. The result is a Carvin catchment that combines French national scale, Belgian product specialisation and immediate seaport access — a structural advantage supporting steady underlying cold-storage demand even before considering the replacement-led demand from the ageing outer-ring (90–120 min) stock identified earlier in this report.

Investment Support Overview

France's inward investment offer is among the strongest in Europe. In 2024 France attracted 1,688 foreign investment decisions creating or saving 37,747 jobs — above pre-COVID levels and confirming its position as one of Europe's most resilient FDI destinations. Within France, Hauts-de-France ranked as the 3rd most attractive region in 2024, with 115 projects and growth of 18.5%, reflecting its strategic positioning between Paris, Belgium and the UK. Nord France Invest provides a dedicated one-stop-shop service — free of charge and confidential — including project management, site search, regulatory navigation, partner introductions and grant signposting. At the national level, Business France provides complementary support, particularly for tax-incentive structuring; it accompanied two-thirds of all FDI decisions in 2024. France's headline corporate income tax rate of 25% is among the lowest in major European economies, supplemented by the Crédit d'Impôt Recherche (CIR) R&D tax credit and France 2030 sector-targeted incentives.

The food and logistics sector is exceptionally well represented: ANIA represents 17,000+ food companies with combined turnover of €197 billion and 450,000+ employees — making French agroalimentaire the country's largest industrial sector and largest industrial employer. The MIN de Lomme — the regional Lille fresh-produce and wholesale food market — sits 25 minutes from Carvin and serves the Lille metropolitan foodservice and retail base. At the national scale, the Rungis International Market, approximately 2 hours south of Carvin via the A1, is the world's largest fresh produce wholesale market, with 1,200 trading companies operating across 232 hectares, 2.4 million tonnes of food delivered annually, an €8.5–10 billion turnover and a daily customer footprint of 18 million consumers — together with the MIN de Lomme representing a ready-made customer prospecting list covering meat, fish, fruit & vegetable wholesalers and 3PL operators serving the Hauts-de-France / Île-de-France corridor.
